Nazanin Zaghari-Ratcliffe: ‘Readjusting to UK life was hard’<\/p>\n\n\n\n

Nazanin Zaghari-Ratcliffe has said readjusting to life in the UK after six years in an Iranian jail had been slower than she had expected.<\/strong><\/p>\n\n\n\n

The British-Iranian national said protests and\u00a0civil unrest in Iran\u00a0since her release had kept her story “fresh on a daily basis”.<\/p>\n\n\n\n

“I resonated very much with what they have gone through,” she said.<\/p>\n\n\n\n

Ms Zaghari-Ratcliffe was arrested in Iran in 2016 on spying charges, which she denied.<\/p>\n\n\n\n

She was released in March 2022 and returned to the UK following a sustained campaign.<\/p>\n\n\n\n

Ms Zaghari-Ratcliffe made the comments on Friday at Glastonbury Festival, where she was giving a talk about Iranian women’s rights.<\/p>\n\n\n\n

Asked what it has been like readjusting to life in the UK, she said: “A lot slower than what I thought it would be.<\/p>\n\n\n\n

“Just because early on when I was released, the uprising in Iran happened… since then I went through stories of many other people who were arrested, and then their stories came out.”<\/p>\n\n\n\n

She discussed the impact of seeing unrest which centred on the death of 22-year-old Mahsa Amini in September while in police custody for wearing her hijab too loosely.<\/p>\n\n\n\n

“My story was all of a sudden so fresh on a daily basis and I couldn’t get myself out of it – it was very hard,” she told the audience.<\/p>\n\n\n\n

“So I think settling down was a lot more complicated and difficult than what I was expecting because of what is happening.<\/p>\n\n\n\n

“But, you know, I can’t complain. I’m free and I’m out, whereas many of my friends are still in prison.”<\/p>\n\n\n\n

During the talk, she said the first names of the friends she was still campaigning to free: Nilufar, Sepideh, Mahvash, Fariba, Morad, Siamak, Emaad and Nargess.<\/p>\n\n\n\n

Ms Zaghari-Ratcliffe, from West Hampstead in London, said she felt the continued unrest in Iran was no longer getting the attention it deserved.<\/p>\n\n\n\n

“I think when this whole uprising happened back in September 2022, there was a lot of momentum, but then, of course, the world moves on,” she said.<\/p>\n\n\n\n

‘Head-in-the-sand’<\/h2>\n\n\n\n

Her husband Richard Ratcliffe also commented on a\u00a0damning report by the Foreign Affairs Committee\u00a0on the Foreign Office’s handling of hostage diplomacy.<\/p>\n\n\n\n

It highlighted examples of “ministerial clumsiness” and “hurtful comments” in its communication with families.<\/p>\n\n\n\n

Mr Ratcliffe described it as an important report.<\/p>\n\n\n\n

“At the moment, cases like Nazanin’s are reasonably rare… but they’re growing, and that growth is something that the government is not really dealing with,” he said.<\/p>\n\n\n\n

“The head-in-the-sand ‘let’s hope we keep this at a low level’ [approach] and manage it like you would with a really rare illness – it doesn’t work.<\/p>\n\n\n\n

“There are a number of countries who are taking hostages, there aren’t many that make the media, there aren’t many that the government will acknowledge as hostages or even acknowledge as arbitrarily detained.<\/p>\n\n\n\n

“We’ll await to see whether the government says ‘yes, hands up, we need to get better’ or whether what we get is a ‘we’ll carry on what we’re currently doing but we’ll tweak around the edges’.<\/p>\n\n\n\n

“I’ve had both in my time… so let’s see what comes.”<\/p>\n\n\n\n

The Foreign Office has been approached for comment.<\/p>\n\n\n\n

Previously, it said: “The best interests of British national detainees is at the heart of our consular work and we support and work with their families wherever we can.”<\/p>\n","protected":false},"excerpt":{"rendered":"
